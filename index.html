Rotation & Revolution

Day & Year

A day is defined as one complete rotation of a planet on its own axis. A year is one complete orbit around the Sun. These two motions are independent — a planet's spin and orbital period are governed by different dynamics.

Uranus spins on its side at a 98° tilt, so each pole faces the Sun for 42 Earth years of continuous daylight — then 42 years of darkness. A baby born at the start of Uranian summer would be middle-aged before the sun sets.
02
Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

Gravitational Force

Every mass attracts every other mass with a force proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Despite being nearly 3 trillion kilometres away, the Sun exerts a colossal pull on Uranus.

$$F = G\,\frac{m_1 \, m_2}{r^2}$$

Gravity grows with mass, but shrinks fast with distance — double the distance and the pull drops to a quarter.

G = constant m₁, m₂ = masses r = separation F = force

Surface Gravity

Gravitational Field

A gravitational field is the region around a massive object where any other mass experiences a gravitational force. Field strength (g) measures the force per unit mass at a given point — it tells us how strongly gravity pulls there.

The Formula
$$g = \frac{G\,m_1}{r^2}$$

"How hard a planet pulls" at any point — depends only on the planet's mass and your distance from its centre.

Gravitational Potential Energy

Work Against Gravity

To lift a 1,000 kg satellite from the surface of Uranus to an altitude of 750 km, we must do work against gravity. Because gravitational force decreases with altitude, we use the change in gravitational potential energy.

Gravitational Potential Energy
$$E_g = -\frac{G\,m_1 m_2}{r}$$
Work Against Gravity
$$W = \Delta E_g = G\,m_1 m_2\!\left(\frac{1}{r_1} - \frac{1}{r_2}\right)$$

The energy required to move an object from one distance to another against gravity — the further you go, the more work is needed.

Overcoming Gravity Permanently

Escape Velocity

Escape velocity is the minimum speed needed to break free from a planet's gravitational field with no further propulsion. It comes from setting kinetic energy equal to the gravitational potential energy needed to reach infinity.

$$v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2\,G\,m_1}{R}}$$

The minimum speed to break free from a planet's gravity forever — no engine, no pushing, just one big launch.

STEP 1
Energy Conservation
Egi + Evi = 0
Evi = −Egi
$$\frac{1}{2}\,m\, v_i^2 = -\!\left(-\frac{G m_1 m_2}{r_i}\right)$$ $$v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2\,G\,m_1}{r_i}}$$
STEP 2
Compute 2Gm₁
2 × 6.673×10−11 × 8.68×1025
= 1.159 × 1016 m3/s2
STEP 3
Divide by R₁
1.159×1016 ÷ 2.56×107
= 4.527 × 108 m2/s2
STEP 4
Square root
√(4.527 × 108) = 21,276 m/s ≈ 21.3 km/s
Uranus escape speed (vesc)
0km/s
To permanently escape Uranus, an object must reach 21.3 km/s — about 63× the speed of sound. Compare: Earth's escape velocity is only 11.2 km/s.
Earth: 11.2 km/s Uranus: 21.3 km/s Uranus is 1.9× harder to escape
◆ WHY IT MATTERS
Anything moving slower than 21.3 km/s falls back to Uranus. That's ~77,000 km/h — about 60× faster than a bullet. For comparison, Earth's escape velocity is only 11.2 km/s.
